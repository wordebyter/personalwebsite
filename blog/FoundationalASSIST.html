Exploring some use cases of the Foundational ASSIST dataset.

Published: April 13, 2026 | Category: Datasets

This post will explore some potential use cases of the Foundational ASSIST dataset, and hopefully aid researchers in figuring out what they hope to investigate with the dataset, and how to do so.

Misconceptions.

We hope our dataset is well suited to analyzing and understanding misconceptions. I'll discuss one potential use case, analyzing the stability of misconceptions in a very small study, but I hope you can build upon the code here to investigate much more interesting questions, much more thoroughly.

There is a long standing belief that misconceptions are stable. This means that given two similar problems, you should expect to see students misconceptions remain stable. Consider the following two problems:

What is the surface area of this rectangular prism? [alt text: 2 by 4 by 4 unit rectangular prism.]
A rectangular prism is 4 units high, 2 units wide, and 6 units long. What is its surface area in square units?

Suppose a student does not understand the difference between volume and surface area. You might then expect a student to answer "32" for the first problem, and "48" for the second.

The results suggest some stability. A lot of students gave both the "32" and "48" answer, but certainly not all. 206 students answered "32" for the first problem, and 98 continued to answer "48" on the second. Thus, I claim misconceptions are about 50% stable. Perhaps this is a weak claim, with very little evidence, but I hope this dataset and example might help others begin to analyze misconceptions. You might also look at whether these misconceptions only persist for a certain period of time, (using interactions['end_time']), across certain skills (using the skills dataset), or across more than 2 problems, using the 3353 unique problems, of which you could likely find many with (or without) persistent, stable, misconceptions.

Knowledge tracing

This was one of the tasks more explicitly defined in the original Foundational ASSIST dataset. We did it on a large scale, but for now lets look at a single student. I selected a random student who has completed 420 problems between September 16, 2021 and February 23, 2022. This is a fairly standard distribution for students in ASSISTments, with students starting in the fall and stopping sometime in the spring. The selected user is an average student, with a correctness of 69%. Their first 5 problems were all multiple choice, and they got the first two ASSISTments problems they did wrong, then got three right.

I'll first note if you ever want to view an image you can go to resources.assistments.org/C/fetch/uuid.jpeg. For example https://resources.assistments.org/fetch/C/81e654f5-2516-45be-9f95-ea8891b4a510.jpeg shows a 2x3 block labeled 'base' and 'height'.

Further, if you ever want to view a problem set, you can use your ASSISTments account and go here: https://student.assistments.org/preview/problemSet/PSABHXCR and replace the PSABHXCR with your problem set id. If you want to view a problem, you can go to https://student.assistments.org/preview/problem/PRABETN5 and replace PRABETN5 with the problem xref (Problem sets start with PS, problems start with PR).

So here's the knowledge tracing question: Do we think the student will get their 6th problem correct?

Knowledge tracing is very rarely done on a small scale, looking at just 6 problems. If I had to guess, I would say yes since they got the past three correct, I think it would be foolish to guess their hotstreak would end. But it is a fill-in, not multiple choice or select all, so maybe that will be harder. But i'd stick with yes. If you want to do knowledge tracing for a specific user over time, this is how you might do so.

I'll also fit a BKT model. The dataset is designed to be LLM friendly, but for now I'll add just a BKT model. We have the code which used LLMs for knowledge tracing on the HuggingFace page if you plan to explore that root.
